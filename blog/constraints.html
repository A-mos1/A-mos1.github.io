Constraints, Not Components

October 2025 · developmental biology, theory

Biology has a paradox at its core that most biologists have quietly agreed to stop thinking about. At the molecular level, gene expression is irreducibly noisy. Cheong and colleagues measured that the TNF-NF-kB pathway transmits roughly 0.92 bits of information per cell per time point (Science, 2011) — barely enough to distinguish signal from silence. Protein copy numbers between genetically identical sister cells, seconds after division, differ by factors of two or three. And yet every human embryo reliably produces hands with five fingers. The same developmental program runs across billions of individuals and deep evolutionary time. Wounds heal. Livers regenerate. The precision of outcomes bears no relationship to the precision of the molecular processes producing them.

The standard answer — genetic determinism, the genome as a blueprint — dissolves under scrutiny. A blueprint specifies outcomes. The genome specifies proteins and their interactions. It encodes components and relationships, not states. Something else is responsible for the reliability.

The real answer has been assembling itself across disconnected fields for seventy years, and it is this: biological systems achieve reliability not by specifying exact states, but by constraining the space of possible trajectories through which those states are reached. The reliability is in the landscape, not the path.

This is the post where that idea gets its full argument. The previous posts in this series documented pieces of it: what sequencing tools can and cannot tell us about how cells move, what the mathematical frameworks for biological dynamics actually say, what recorder tools are beginning to make visible, and why organ engineering keeps failing at the self-organization step. The argument here is that those pieces share a common structure, and that recognizing the structure changes what the field should be building toward.

The landscape is real, not a metaphor

Waddington drew his epigenetic landscape in 1957: a ball rolling down branching valleys, each valley a cell fate. He called the robustness of developmental trajectories "canalization" — the tendency of development to reach the same endpoint despite perturbation — and he even drew the underside of his diagram, genes as pegs connected by strings to the surface above, intuiting that regulatory architecture shapes the landscape topology. This was a metaphor without mathematics. But the intuition was correct, and it seeded the theoretical work that followed.

Kauffman formalized the attractor idea in 1969 (Journal of Theoretical Biology): cell types as stable attractors of Boolean gene regulatory networks, not individual programs but natural fixed points of the regulatory dynamics. The exact scaling he proposed was later corrected by Samuelsson and Troein (Physical Review Letters, 2003), but the core claim held — cell types emerge from regulatory network dynamics, they are not specified one by one. Barkai and Leibler (Nature, 1997) showed that robust adaptation in bacterial chemotaxis is a structural property of network topology, not fine-tuned parameters. Yi, Huang, Simon, and Doyle (PNAS, 2000) proved that what Barkai and Leibler discovered is literally integral feedback control — the same mathematical operation in a cruise controller.

The most complete mathematical version came from Jin Wang and colleagues (PNAS, 2008 onward). They showed that biological dynamics are governed by two forces: a gradient force from a quasi-potential landscape (U = −ln P_ss) and a rotational curl flux that drives probability currents through the landscape, breaking time-reversal symmetry and making differentiation directional. The clean Boltzmann-like formula P_ss ∝ exp(−U/D) holds only for gradient systems at detailed balance. Living cells are driven, dissipative, far-from-equilibrium. The flux is not a correction — it is the difference between a physics model and a biology model. Optimal paths for differentiation deviate from naive gradient descent because of the flux, and may not pass through the saddle points equilibrium theory predicts. Wang's group validated this against actual single-cell transcriptomic data in PNAS (2024).

The clearest concrete demonstration that biological networks implement control laws — not just produce outputs — is the Drosophila gap gene network. Bicoid and Nanos establish opposing morphogen gradients along the anterior-posterior axis of the embryo. These gradients are noisy: Gregor and colleagues (Cell, 2007) measured that Bicoid concentration varies by roughly 10% between embryos. Yet the position of the Hunchback boundary — the sharp line where gap gene expression switches from high to low — varies by less than 1% of embryo length across individuals. The network is not simply reading a threshold. It is computing something closer to a derivative: integrating concentration, rate of change, and neighbor state simultaneously to produce a boundary sharper than the input gradient. That is a transfer function operating in biological tissue. The reliability does not come from precise morphogen concentrations. It comes from the network architecture that filters noise and enforces the boundary regardless of how much the input wobbles.

A second example, closer to organ engineering: YAP and TAZ in epithelial sheets. Cells in a monolayer collectively maintain homeostatic cell density through a mechanical feedback loop — crowding increases compressive stress, YAP is excluded from the nucleus, growth arrest follows, density drops, stress relaxes, YAP re-enters, growth resumes. The set point is mechanical stress. The error signal is deviation from it. The actuator is YAP nuclear localization and the downstream transcriptional program. Eisenhoffer and colleagues (Nature, 2012) showed that even when individual cells are ablated by laser, neighbors sense the local tension change within minutes and fill the gap. The control law runs continuously and responds to inputs that are not chemical but mechanical. This is the same architectural principle as the gap gene network — a network computing a relationship between sensed deviations and corrective outputs — operating across a completely different physical domain.

The landscape is measurable in principle: the negative log probability of finding a cell in each state, shaped by the regulatory architecture, with probability currents flowing through it. But measuring it requires observing trajectories of individual cells navigating the landscape — many cells, the same landscape, different paths to the same fate or different fates from similar starting points. That data does not exist. We have snapshots.

What snapshots cannot tell you

Single-cell RNA sequencing provides a 20,000-dimensional molecular snapshot of a cell at the moment it is lysed. Pseudotime infers ordering. RNA velocity infers direction. Optimal transport infers the most likely population-level redistribution between time points. These methods extract real signal and they are genuinely useful. But Weinreb, Wolock, and Klein (PNAS, 2018) proved the fundamental limit: for any observed distribution of cell states, multiple distinct dynamics could have generated it. The snapshot underdetermines the trajectory. This is a mathematical theorem, not a technical limitation awaiting a better algorithm.

What this means for the constraint framework is specific. The landscape is encoded in how individual cells move: which states follow which, with what probability, under what perturbations, at what timescales. Population-level snapshots average over this structure. They tell you where cells tend to be, not why they got there or what prevented them from going elsewhere. The constraint — the gap between the space of all possible states and the space of states actually visited — is precisely what disappears in the averaging.

Live-cell biosensors show what is being missed. ERK does not simply activate in response to EGF — it pulses, oscillates, and adapts, with frequency-modulated encoding that carries substantially more than one bit. NF-kB oscillates with a roughly 90-minute period (Nelson et al., Science, 2004), constant across stimulation intensities. Transient versus sustained ERK activation drives opposing cell fate decisions through the same pathway. The signaling code is written in time. A tool that reads one frame reads noise.

Live imaging gets you closer to the dynamics but runs into two walls. The first is multiplexing: you can watch YAP shuttle in and out of the nucleus, or ERK pulse, or calcium spike — one or two labeled proteins at a time in a system with dozens of coupled inputs. You are watching one thread of a process that only makes sense as a pattern across many threads simultaneously. The second wall is more fundamental for anyone working toward tissue engineering: live imaging does not scale to three-dimensional tissue at the resolution needed for molecular identity. You get the movie or the endpoint readout, not both. Watch the cells live and you cannot sequence them at the end. Sequence them and you destroyed the movie.

The recorder tools described in the previous post — ENGRAM, BASELINE, CytoTape, TimeVault — are the attempt to break out of that constraint. Rather than watching in real time, they write the history into the cell itself, readable after the fact alongside molecular identity. But even combined, they store a few thousand bits where a developmental trajectory contains millions. The gap is three to five orders of magnitude. The timescale range relevant to cell fate spans seven decades, from ion channel gating to fate commitment. No existing tool covers more than two of those decades at single-cell molecular resolution.

The argument

Here is the claim I want to make, carefully.

The field of cell and developmental biology has been operating under an implicit assumption: that if we measure molecular state with sufficient resolution, dynamics will emerge. More genes, more cells, more time points. The snapshot accumulates into understanding. This assumption is wrong in a specific way. Dynamics are not in the snapshots. They are in the structure connecting snapshots — the topology of the landscape, the flux, the noise at bifurcation points, the constraints that make certain trajectories probable and others forbidden. Collecting more snapshots of dead cells does not reveal this structure, because the structure is about how cells move, not where they sit.

The correct object of study is the trajectory, and the correct question is: what constraints shaped it? Not what genes are expressed, but which states followed which others, in which individual cells, under which perturbations, across which timescales. The genome encodes the constraints. The regulatory architecture instantiates them. The trajectory is what the constraints permit.

This is also the argument against the current generation of foundation models trained on static transcriptomes. A control law — the rule a network uses to correct deviation and enforce a setpoint — only exists as a relationship between variables over time. It is a transfer function, not a state. You cannot reconstruct a transfer function from a single output measurement: this is mathematically impossible for the same reason you cannot identify a differential equation from one data point. Models trained on tens of millions of snapshots of dead cells learn correlational structure across states. They cannot learn causal structure across time, because time is not in the data. The gap gene network does not care what Hunchback expression level looks like in a cross-sectional survey. It cares what Bicoid is doing right now and what its neighbors are doing right now and how those inputs have been changing over the last few minutes. That is the computation. Snapshots cannot encode it.

This reframes what the measurement challenge actually is. It is not primarily a throughput problem or a sensitivity problem. It is a longitudinal, multi-modal, multi-timescale problem. You need to watch the same cell over time, record the dynamic events that constitute its history, and integrate that information across the hierarchy from second-scale signaling to week-scale fate commitment. The tools that do pieces of this — biosensors, metabolic labeling, molecular recorders, non-destructive transcriptomics — need to be layered together in the same cells, registered to the same spatial coordinates, and read out at compatible time points. That integrated architecture does not yet exist. Building it is the work.

What would it give us? The Waddington landscape as an empirical object — measurable from trajectory data, not imposed as a model assumption. Bifurcation points identifiable from the actual divergence of trajectories, not inferred from clustering boundaries. Noise structure quantified at each decision point, distinguishing intrinsic stochasticity from extrinsic variation from functionally exploited noise. Perturbation effects evaluable as landscape reshaping — does this drug flatten a barrier, eliminate an attractor, create a new basin? — rather than as average state shifts. And crucially: causal structure visible from the temporal order of events in individual cells, something that no static measurement, however high-dimensional, can provide.

Why this matters for organ engineering specifically

An earlier post in this series laid out the honest state of organ bioengineering. The clinical wins are for avascular tissues with simple architectures. The persistent failures are for anything requiring self-organization at scale. The bottlenecks — vascularization, cell maturation, innervation — are real, but there is a sixth bottleneck that rarely gets named as such: we cannot monitor or guide self-organization as it happens.

Organoids prove that cells know how to self-organize. A single intestinal stem cell embedded in the right matrix rebuilds crypt-villus architecture (Sato and Clevers, Nature, 2009). The self-organizing capacity is encoded in the regulatory network. What fails in organoid engineering is not the capacity but the reliability. Batch-to-batch variation, incomplete patterning, maturation that stalls at fetal levels — these reflect sensitivity to early stochastic events that propagate into divergent developmental outcomes. By the time you can see the problem, the critical decisions are already made.

The constraint framework says something precise about this failure mode. Early developmental decisions are bifurcation points — moments when the trajectory crosses a basin boundary and commits to one attractor rather than another. The noise at those points determines how stochastic the commitment is. The boundary conditions — growth factors, mechanical context, cell-cell signaling — determine where the boundary sits. If you could monitor the signaling dynamics and transcriptional state of cells during the early hours of organoid development, you would see the bifurcation events as they happen. You could identify which cells are straying from the desired trajectory, what signal they are missing or receiving in excess, and intervene before the deviation propagates.

This is feedback control of self-organization — not specifying every outcome, but maintaining the boundary conditions that keep the system within the basin of attraction of the desired developmental trajectory. The landscape does the rest. Khammash's group at ETH has demonstrated closed-loop feedback control of gene expression in living cells (Nature Communications, 2021), maintaining defined set points despite perturbation through integral feedback implemented in optogenetics. Ben-Zvi and Barkai (PNAS, 2010) showed that morphogen gradient scaling during organ growth already uses integral feedback natively. The control architecture exists in principle. What it lacks is the measurement layer.

What this field needs to build

The integrated measurement architecture has no single-sentence description because it is not a single tool. It is a stack: fast biosensors for signaling dynamics at the second-to-minute scale, metabolic labeling for transcriptional rates at the minute-to-hour scale, protein assembly recorders like CytoTape and GEMINI for multi-week transcription factor histories, molecular recorders like ENGRAM and BASELINE for lineage and signal history at the day-to-week scale, and spatial transcriptomics registered to the same cells throughout. The individual layers exist in prototype. The integration does not.

The computational frameworks are ready for this data. Neural stochastic differential equations (scDiffEq, Vinyard et al., Nature Machine Intelligence, 2025) model both deterministic drift and state-dependent diffusion from trajectory observations. Schrödinger bridge methods find the most probable continuous process connecting observed cell distributions at discrete time points. Path-integral methods for empirical landscape reconstruction have been demonstrated by Cislo and Siggia (PNAS, 2025). These methods consistently perform better when given lineage-traced trajectory data rather than unlinked snapshots. The bottleneck is not algorithmic.

Foundation models trained on tens of millions of static single-cell profiles — scGPT, Geneformer, scFoundation — have absorbed substantial biological correlational structure. A 2025 sparse autoencoder analysis found that only 3 of 48 tested transcription factors showed regulatory-target-specific feature responses. These models learn co-expression, not causation. This is not a failure of architecture. It is a consequence of data. You cannot learn causal dynamics from snapshots, and scaling the number of snapshots does not help. The data generates the model, and static data generates static models.

The observation that matters here is simple: the theoretical frameworks have been waiting for trajectory data for decades. Waddington intuited the landscape in 1957. Wang derived it from first principles in 2008. The tools to measure it are becoming available now. What connects them is the recognition that the landscape is not a metaphor or a model assumption — it is an empirical object, measurable from trajectory data, that encodes exactly the information needed to understand why cells make the fate decisions they make and to guide the self-organization that organ engineering requires.

The closing point

Seventy years of theory have been telling biology that it is a dynamical system defined by constraints on trajectories, not inventories of components. The measurement technology has not caught up. The single-cell revolution brought us close and then produced a ceiling: extraordinary resolution on what cells contain at a single instant, no information about how they got there or where they are going.

The ceiling is not permanent. Recorders are gaining orders of magnitude in information capacity per generation. Protein assembly systems record weeks of transcription factor dynamics in living mouse neurons. Non-destructive transcriptomics is becoming feasible. Spatial multi-omics is becoming routine. The computational frameworks are built and waiting. What remains is building the integrated architecture that layers these tools together, applying it to developing organoids and tissues, and using the resulting trajectory data to reconstruct the empirical landscapes that developmental biology has been theorizing about since Waddington drew his hillside.

The cells know how to build organs. Every embryo proves this. The question is whether we can learn enough about the constraints they are navigating to keep them on the right trajectory when we ask them to do it outside the body. That is a measurement problem before it is an engineering problem. And the measurement problem has a specific shape: we need not just the output state but the integrated history — what signals the cell accumulated, what forces it felt, what its neighbors were doing, what trajectories it moved through — readable alongside its molecular identity at the end. That is what lets you reconstruct the transfer function rather than just observe that the system stayed on course. Not the outcome. The rule it used to get there.
